INTRODUCTION  The wood  of Ocotea catharinensis  Mez is a much ap-  preciated  article of commerce  [2). The tree, popularly  known as ‘canela preta’, occurs in the Atlantic forest. A  specimen was collected in the Cunha region, %o Paulo  State, and  identified  by Professor  Klaus  Kubitzki,  University of Hamburg. Fractionation  of the light petrol  extract of its bark gave the neolignans la, lb, Za, 2b, 3a, 4a  and 5a. The analysed samples of  the latter species were originally designated  A. simulans  Allen [6], an error which was recently corrected [7]. We  have indicated  previously that compounds  of type 2  should be artifacts of isolation [8]. Indeed, the putative  precursors of 2a, namely la and lb, are also present  in  the  extract  of  0.  catharinensis.  As expected  [S],  compound  la in chloroform  is demethylated  into 2a in  561  562  M. HARAGUCH~ et al.  the presence  of a trace of acid at room  temperature.  Compounds  la and lb show very similar IR and mass  spectra.  According  to their ‘H and 13C NMR data they  differ  only  with  respect  to the configuration  of the  hydroxyl  group at C-4’, endo-oriented  in la (y-effects on  C-7 and C-6’ for la vs 1 beach 6 - 2.0) and exo-oriented  in  lb (y-effect on C-2’ for lb vs la 64.2).  Acetylation  (acetic anhydride,  pyridine)  at room tem-  perature  transformed  la into the monoacetate  lc, and lb  into the diacetate  le. Complete  acetylation  of la into Id  required  heating. The monoacetate  lc is constitutionally  identical with 6, a compound  isolated from Ocotea sp. [S].  Stereochemically  the compounds  differ in the configur-  ations of the aryl group at C-7 and the methyl group at  C-8. The former is endo-situated  in 6, exerting protection  on the acetate  protons  (61.54) and exo-situated  in lc  (acetate,  62.14).
